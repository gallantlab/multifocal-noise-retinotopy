Beginner's guide

What this tool makes and what every setting does, explained from scratch — no background in vision or neuroscience needed. For the operating manual and technical reference, see the User's guide.

What this is

This tool makes short movies used in brain-imaging experiments. The movies are played to a person lying in an MRI scanner so researchers can figure out how that person's visual brain is wired — a standard technique called retinotopic mapping. You don't need to understand the brain side to use the tool; this page explains every idea from the ground up.

A finished movie is just a folder of images (one per frame) plus a few summary pictures and a data file describing exactly what was shown.

In one sentence

We carve a circle into pieces, flicker each piece with colourful textured static on a clever schedule, and record the brain — and from that we can reconstruct a map of the person's visual world.

The big picture

The screen shows a gray background with a small dot in the centre that the viewer stares at the whole time (this is the fixation spot). Centred on that dot is a disc (a filled circle). The disc is divided into regions, and each region switches between showing textured noise and showing nothing (just background), over and over, on its own schedule.

Why bother? The back of your brain holds an orderly map of what you see: neighbouring spots in the world light up neighbouring spots of brain. To measure that map, two things about each point in the visual field must be pinned down:

  • Its angle — where it sits around the clock-face of your vision (3 o'clock? straight up?).
  • Its eccentricity — how far out from the centre of gaze it is (right next to where you're looking, or far out in the corner of your eye).

The two region shapes this tool offers each target one of those:

Wedges vs. rings

wedges measure angle

The disc is cut into pie slices. Each slice covers one range of angles around the centre. Lighting up slices in turn tells you which part of the brain handles each direction in the visual field (polar-angle mapping).

Think: a pizza, or the hours on a clock.

rings measure distance

The disc is cut into concentric circles (like a dartboard). Each ring covers one band of distances from the centre. Lighting up rings in turn tells you which part of the brain handles each eccentricity (how far out) — this is eccentricity mapping.

Think: a dartboard, or ripples from a dropped stone.

You pick one with the stimulus control at the top of the form. Everything else in the tool works exactly the same for both — the only difference is the region shape. (Because of that, a few controls keep "wedge" in their name even in rings mode; the form relabels them to "ring" for you.)

Key ideas, explained

The on/off schedule (m-sequence & "multifocal")

The obvious way to map the brain is to show one region at a time. That works but is slow. Instead this tool turns all regions on and off at once, each following its own on/off schedule, and untangles them afterward in software. That "everybody at once" approach is called a multifocal design.

The schedules come from a mathematical pattern called an m-sequence (short for maximum-length sequence) — a string of 0s and 1s with a special property: it barely resembles shifted copies of itself. Each region uses a different shift of the same sequence, so no two regions share a pattern, and the analysis can cleanly separate each region's contribution from a single recording. The schedule has 63 steps; each step is called a state.

why it's clever

Because the schedules are nearly uncorrelated, testing 8 (or 63) regions simultaneously is almost as informative as testing each alone — but many times faster.

Why textured "noise", and what 1/f means

Each on region isn't filled with a solid colour — it's filled with noise: a colourful, finely textured static. Rich texture drives far more of the visual brain than a flat patch, which makes the recording more informative (and lets researchers fit detailed "encoding models" of what each bit of brain prefers).

The specific recipe is 1/f noise. That's the same statistical signature found in ordinary photographs: plenty of large, smooth features and progressively fewer tiny ones. It looks natural rather than harsh. ("1/f" just means the amount of texture falls off as the features get finer.) You can switch this to flat (every feature size equally strong) if you prefer.

Two kinds of "frequency": spatial and temporal

"Frequency" sounds technical but here it means two simple things:

  • Spatial frequency = how fine the texture is. Low = big coarse blobs; high = fine, detailed grain. You set a range (a low and a high cutoff) so the texture contains feature sizes only within that band. Units are cycles across the width — e.g. 2 means a feature that repeats twice across the whole disc (very coarse); 128 is fine detail.
  • Temporal frequency = how fast the texture shimmers over time. Low = slow drift; high = fast flicker. Measured in Hz (changes per second). You again set a low and a high cutoff.

handy

If you change the movie width, the tool automatically rescales the spatial-frequency band so the texture keeps the same look (and stays valid). See Things to watch for.

Orientation (which way the texture "points")

The noise isn't just blobby — it's given a direction, like faint stripes. Orientation is the angle of those stripes: 0° = horizontal, 90° = vertical. The visual brain cares a lot about orientation, so it's a useful extra dimension to probe.

Crucially, each on-region's orientation is set by a second, independent m-sequence. So every active region carries two separable pieces of information at once: whether it's on, and which way its texture points. The # orientations control chooses how many equally-spaced directions are in play (2 → horizontal & vertical; 4 → adds the two diagonals; etc.).

The oriented_mseq background (see below) adds a third m-sequence (its own separate schedule) that points the whole background in its own direction each state, largely uncorrelated with the regions.

one thing worth knowing

Every direction is built the same way — from a thin slice of nearby directions in the maths behind the scenes. Horizontal and vertical could be made mathematically perfect (razor-sharp stripes), but then they'd look cleaner than the diagonals, and a region might stand out by looking smoother rather than by its orientation. Building every direction identically keeps the only difference the one you care about: orientation.

The background behind the regions

What fills the disc around the active regions is up to you:

  • gray — plain flat gray (the simplest, most neutral choice).
  • random — full-screen textured noise with no particular direction (all orientations mixed).
  • oriented — full-screen directional noise whose angle is chosen, each state, to point as differently as possible from the active regions' stripes. This keeps the regions "popping out" from the background by orientation.
  • oriented_mseq — full-screen directional noise whose angle follows its own independent schedule (a separate m-sequence), set to angles that sit halfway between the regions' angles (e.g. regions at 0°/90° → background at 45°/135°). Unlike oriented, its angle doesn't depend on which regions happen to be on, so the background always cycles through a full, even set of directions.

The fixation spot

A tiny 2×2-pixel square at the exact centre that the viewer fixates on throughout. It changes to a random colour twice a second, which gives the viewer a little task (helping them hold their gaze steady) and marks the centre. Its colour sequence is recorded so you know exactly what was shown.

Every control, explained

Defaults are in (). Controls that only matter for one geometry are tagged wedges or rings.

stimulus (wedges)
Region shape: wedges (pie slices → angle map) or rings (concentric circles → distance map). Everything else is identical between them.
movie width (pix) (512)
Size of the square movie in pixels (so 512 = 512×512). Bigger = sharper but slower to make and larger files.
# wedges / # rings (8)
How many regions the disc is split into. More regions = a finer map but each is smaller. Capped at 63 (the schedule length). Ring 1 is the innermost.
wedges / rings shown (all)
Tick boxes to show only some regions (with all / none / alternate shortcuts). Hidden regions simply never turn on. Handy for, say, presenting every other ring.
wedge rotation (deg) wedges (22.5)
Rotates where the slice boundaries fall, without changing anything else. The default 22.5° shifts every cut by half a slice, so no boundary sits exactly on the horizontal or vertical.
ring spacing rings (log)
How the rings are spaced from centre to edge:
  • log — thin near the centre, thick toward the edge. This matches how the brain devotes more area to central vision, and is the usual choice.
  • equal_width — every ring the same thickness on screen.
  • equal_area — every ring the same screen area (so they get thinner outward).
wedge / ring duration (sec) (4.1)
How long each of the 63 states is shown before moving to the next.
frame rate (hz) (30)
Frames shown per second — the movie's playback/refresh speed.
color / BW (color)
color gives independently coloured texture (each fine feature a random colour); bw gives grayscale.
temporal frequency: shape, lowest, highest (1/f, 0.5–15 Hz)
How fast the texture changes over time. shape is 1/f (natural-looking) or flat; lowest/highest set the flicker-speed band in Hz.
spatial frequency: shape, lowest, highest (1/f, 2–128 cyc/width)
How fine the texture is. lowest/highest set the range of feature sizes, in cycles across the width (small = coarse, large = fine). Changing the movie width auto-rescales these so the look is preserved.
# orientations (2)
How many equally-spaced stripe directions the textures can take. 2 → horizontal & vertical; 4 → add the diagonals.
background (gray)
gray, random (undirected noise), oriented (directional noise pointed away from the active regions), or oriented_mseq (directional noise on its own independent schedule, angles interleaved with the regions'). See above.
fade between states (frames) (5)
Each region eases on and off over this many frames instead of snapping, so the movie isn't jarring.
padding before/after (sec) (2)
Seconds of plain full-screen noise added at the start and end — useful as lead-in / lead-out for a scanner run.
fixation spot (on)
Turns the central colour-changing dot on or off.
demo / full (demo)
demo renders just the first 5 states for a quick preview; full renders all 63 (the real experiment — much slower).

Things to watch for

A few real limits, in plain terms:

  • You can't flicker faster than half the frame rate. At 30 frames/s the fastest meaningful flicker is 15 Hz. Asking for more just creates artefacts. (This is the "Nyquist" limit.)
  • You can't show detail finer than the pixels allow. The finest texture is set by the movie width; the highest spatial frequency caps at half the width.
  • Every orientation is built the same way. Each direction (horizontal, vertical, and diagonal alike) is built from the same thin slice of directions. Horizontal and vertical could be made mathematically perfect, but that would make them look like clean stripes while diagonals look grainy — so a region could stand out by its texture rather than its orientation. Treating them all the same keeps the comparison fair.
  • Neighbouring regions show a seam. Each region's noise is independent (its own random seed), so two adjacent regions don't blend — this is intentional and keeps them separable.
  • Fades go to the background, not across states. Between states the screen returns to the background; states don't cross-dissolve into each other.
  • Same settings → identical movie. The randomness is seeded, so regenerating with the same parameters reproduces the exact same movie.

How many background orientations will I get?

With the oriented background, its angle is recomputed each state to point away from whatever orientations the active regions are showing. How many distinct background angles appear over a run depends on the number of regions and the number of orientations:

With the defaults (8 regions, 2 orientations) you get 3 background orientations: 0°, 45°, 90°. Note the surprising twist: more regions gives fewer background orientations, because with many regions on at once it's likely all orientations are present, and that always maps to the single "in-between" angle. If you want the background to vary, use fewer regions or more orientations.

The table above is only for the oriented background. The oriented_mseq background avoids this entirely: it has its own schedule, so it always cycles through a full, even set of K orientations (the angles halfway between the regions' angles), no matter how many regions are on.

What comes out

Generating a movie writes, next to the program:

  • frames/frame_00000.png, …00001.png, … — the movie itself, one image per frame.
  • movie_meta.json — a complete record of everything that was shown (geometry, the full on/off and orientation schedules for all 63 states, background angles, fixation colours, timing, and all settings). Enough to reconstruct the experiment exactly.
  • design_matrix.png / orientation_matrix.png — at-a-glance pictures of the on/off and orientation schedules (states down, regions across).
  • temporal_spectrum.png, spatial_spectrum.png, orientation_spectrum.png — measured-vs-ideal checks that the noise really has the texture you asked for.

The viewer on the main page plays the movie and shows all of these together, with a moving cursor that tracks which state is on screen.
